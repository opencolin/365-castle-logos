### Query
von willebrand disease

### Answer
Von Willebrand disease (VWD) is an inherited bleeding disorder where blood does not clot properly because the von Willebrand factor (VWF) protein is missing, low, or not functioning normally.
VWF is a protein that helps platelets stick together at a site of injury and also stabilizes clotting factor VIII.
In VWD, there is a quantitative deficiency of VWF (too little) or a qualitative defect (it does not work correctly), so clots form more slowly and bleeding can last longer than normal.
There are three major types of VWD.
Type 1: Partial quantitative deficiency of VWF, usually autosomal dominant, most common and often mild.
Type 2: Qualitative defects in VWF function, with subtypes 2A, 2B, 2M, and 2N that differ in how VWF structure or interactions are altered.
Type 3: Near-complete or complete absence of VWF, autosomal recessive, rare and usually severe with very low factor VIII levels.
Many people have mild or no symptoms; others may have significant mucocutaneous bleeding.

Common features include:
Easy bruising and frequent nosebleeds  
Prolonged bleeding from cuts, dental work, or surgery  
Gum bleeding and prolonged bleeding after childbirth or miscarriage  
Heavy menstrual bleeding (large clots, changing pads/tampons more than hourly, needing double protection) and iron‑deficiency anemia in some women
In severe forms (especially type 3), spontaneous bleeding into joints or soft tissue with pain and swelling.
VWD is usually caused by pathogenic variants in the VWF gene and is typically inherited, affecting both males and females.
Type 1 and most type 2 forms are autosomal dominant (one altered gene copy is enough), while type 3 and some type 2 variants are autosomal recessive (two altered copies required).
There is also an acquired von Willebrand syndrome that can develop later in life from other medical conditions, but this is less common.
Diagnosis uses:
Bleeding history and family history  
Screening tests (like aPTT, factor VIII level)  
Specific assays for VWF antigen, VWF activity (ristocetin cofactor or similar), and sometimes multimer analysis and genetic tests.

Management depends on type and severity, and may include:
Desmopressin (DDAVP) to raise VWF and factor VIII levels in many type 1 and some type 2 patients  
VWF‑containing factor concentrates for those who do not respond to DDAVP or have severe disease  
Antifibrinolytic agents (e.g., tranexamic acid) for mucosal bleeding or dental work  
Hormonal therapies or other measures for heavy menstrual bleeding.
