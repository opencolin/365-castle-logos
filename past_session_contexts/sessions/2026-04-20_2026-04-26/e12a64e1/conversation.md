### Answer
Lahore Fort is a Mughal citadel in Lahore, Pakistan whose current brick-and-stone form was begun in 1566 under Emperor Akbar, on the site of an older mud fort.
In 1566 Akbar ordered the demolition of the earlier mud-brick fort and rebuilt it in brick masonry and red sandstone, creating the foundations of the fort complex we see today.
This work turned Lahore into a key military and administrative stronghold on the Mughal Empire’s north‑western frontier, guarding routes toward Kabul, Multan, and Kashmir.
The style Akbar used was deliberately syncretic, mixing Islamic and Hindu architectural motifs, which you still see in features like elephant‑bracket columns and carved sandstone details.
Shah Jahan later transformed parts of the fort into a pleasure residence, adding structures such as the Sheesh Mahal (Palace of Mirrors), Naulakha Pavilion, and Moti Masjid in the 17th century.
Emperor Aurangzeb built the monumental Alamgiri Gate on the western side in 1674; this is now the main iconic entrance facing the Badshahi Mosque.
Further modifications came under the Sikh rulers (notably Ranjit Singh’s pavilions) and the British, so the fort is a palimpsest of Mughal, Sikh, and colonial interventions.
Lahore Fort covers over 20 hectares at the north‑western corner of Lahore’s walled city and is tightly integrated with surrounding monuments like the Badshahi Mosque and Hazuri Bagh.
UNESCO inscribed the fort as a World Heritage Site in 1981 for its outstanding ensemble of Mughal-period monuments and decorative arts (such as the Picture Wall’s tile mosaics and frescoes).
